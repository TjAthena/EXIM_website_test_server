Informationen über Erdnüsse

Erdnüsse, auch bekannt als Erdnüsse, sind eine Hülsenfrucht, die für ihre Nährstoffdichte und Vielseitigkeit in verschiedenen kulinarischen Anwendungen bekannt ist. Sie sind technisch gesehen Hülsenfrüchte, keine Nüsse, und gehören zur Familie der Fabaceae. Erdnüsse können roh, geröstet oder als Zutaten in Erdnussbutter, Kochölen und Saucen verwendet werden. Sie sind besonders beliebt in Snacks und als Grundnahrungsmittel in vielen Küchen weltweit.

Peanut for Birds

Peanut Supply Chain: From the Farm to the Feeder

Cultivation & Harvesting

In India, peanuts intended for bird feed are primarily grown in Gujarat, Rajasthan, Andhra Pradesh, and Tamil Nadu. The main varieties include Runner/Bold and Spanish/Java, influenced by climate, soil, and agricultural techniques.

Initial Sorting & Cleaning

Peanuts are sorted to remove dirt and contaminants using specialized machinery. Only high-quality peanuts, sized 60/70, 80/100, or split, are selected to avoid health risks for birds, such as choking hazards.

Processing

Peanuts are blanched, roasted, or crushed to enhance their flavor, nutritional value, and shelf life. Roasting and crushing make them easier to consume and digest for birds, especially smaller species.

Quality Control & Grading

Peanuts undergo strict aflatoxin testing to meet safety standards. Quality control ensures that peanuts are free from harmful contaminants, ensuring a safe and healthy product for bird food.

Packaging & Distribution

Peanuts are packaged in various forms, including bulk bags (1 to 1.25 tons) and vacuum packs of 25 kg. Distribution channels ensure that the product reaches retailers, meeting the needs of gardeners and wild bird feeders.


Price Comparison
Bird Peanuts vs. Human Grade Peanuts

Peanuts intended for bird feed are generally more affordable than human-grade peanuts, although the prices are linked. A decrease in human-grade peanut prices often leads to reduced costs for bird peanuts due to factors like supply and yield. Human-grade peanuts have stricter aflatoxin limits (Aflatoxin B1: 2 ppb, Total: 4 ppb), while bird peanuts must meet a less stringent European standard of total aflatoxin below 20 ppb, highlighting the need for safety testing.
